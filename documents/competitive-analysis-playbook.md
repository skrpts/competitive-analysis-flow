## Competitive Analysis Playbook

This playbook guides you through running a thorough competitive analysis using the Competitive Analysis Flow. Follow it step by step for your first analysis; adapt it to your needs once you are familiar with the process.

### Before You Start

**Gather your inputs.** The pipeline produces better results with richer context. Prepare:

- A clear description of your product and its primary value proposition
- The market segment you compete in (be specific — "project management for engineering teams" is better than "productivity software")
- Your target customer profile (company size, industry, buyer role)
- Any competitors you already know about
- Recent win/loss data if available (which competitors appeared in deals, and whether you won or lost)

**Set your analysis scope.** Decide upfront:
- How many competitors to include (5-8 is manageable; more than 12 becomes unwieldy)
- Which comparison dimensions matter most to your buyers
- Who the audience for the final brief is (executive team, product team, sales team)
- Whether you need a full analysis or an update to a previous one

### Running the Pipeline

**Stage 1: Competitor Discovery**

Provide your product description, market segment, and any known competitors. The pipeline will identify additional competitors across three tiers.

Review the output critically:
- Are the direct competitors genuinely competing for the same budget?
- Have any obvious competitors been missed?
- Are the emerging threats realistic or speculative?

Add or remove competitors before proceeding. It is much cheaper to adjust the list now than after building the full comparison matrix.

**Stage 2: Feature Comparison**

The pipeline builds a multi-dimensional comparison matrix. This is the most data-intensive stage.

Tips for better results:
- Provide specific feature information about your product — the pipeline cannot assess what it does not know about
- If you have access to competitor products (free trials, demos), note specific observations
- Share any recent win/loss feedback that mentions feature gaps or advantages

Review the comparison matrix for accuracy. If a score seems wrong, provide counter-evidence and re-run.

**Stage 3: SWOT Analysis**

The SWOT builds on the comparison matrix. Review it for honesty:
- Do the weaknesses feel real, or has the analysis been too kind?
- Are the threats genuinely threatening, or are they distant hypotheticals?
- Do the opportunities align with your product's actual capabilities?

The cross-quadrant analysis is the most valuable part. Pay particular attention to weakness-threat compounds — these are your biggest strategic risks.

**Stage 4: Positioning Map**

The positioning map requires two strategic dimensions. Review the selected dimensions:
- Do they matter to your buyers?
- Do they differentiate competitors (spread them out rather than cluster them)?
- Would your sales team recognise these as the dimensions customers evaluate?

If the dimensions feel wrong, suggest alternatives and re-run.

**Stage 5: Competitive Brief**

The final brief compiles everything into an executive-ready document. Before sharing:
- Verify the executive summary accurately reflects the full analysis
- Check that recommendations are actionable and appropriately prioritised
- Ensure the tone is balanced — neither complacent nor alarmist

### After the Analysis

**Share selectively.** Different audiences need different levels of detail:
- Executives: Executive summary + recommendations
- Product team: Full brief + feature comparison
- Sales team: Competitor profiles + positioning + battle cards (derived from the brief)

**Schedule a refresh.** Competitive landscapes change. Set a calendar reminder:
- Fast-moving markets (AI, SaaS): Refresh quarterly
- Moderate markets (B2B software): Refresh every six months
- Stable markets: Refresh annually

**Track changes.** When refreshing, compare against the previous analysis. What has changed? Which predictions came true? Which competitors have moved? This trend analysis becomes increasingly valuable over time.

### Common Mistakes

1. **Confirmation bias.** If your analysis shows your product is the best at everything, you have not looked hard enough. Every product has weaknesses.
2. **Feature fixation.** Products compete on more than features. Pricing, brand, community, and distribution all matter.
3. **Static thinking.** Competitors move. A competitor that is weak today might be investing heavily and be strong in 12 months. Look at trajectories, not just current state.
4. **Analysis paralysis.** A good-enough analysis delivered this week is more valuable than a perfect analysis delivered next month. Do not let the quest for completeness prevent action.
